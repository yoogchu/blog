machine learning

So I recently crammed for my machine learning midterm by watching a zillion videos of my professor talk about the different algorithms and learning in general. It was actually quite entertaining, despite being sleep deprived and half delirious half the time. In the beginning of the semester, I actually really disliked the class, mostly because I didn’t understand what was happening. Eventually as I began to understand the nuances in machine learning and spot trends in the datasets I was analyzing, it because extremely fascinating.

In my last project on randomized optimization, it was fascinating to examine different, extremely well-known optimization problems such as the Knapsack problem and the Travelling Salesman Problem because they’re actually problems that people face on a daily basis. By analyzing data and applying these algorithms on them, it absolutely amazed me that it could return objectively correct solutions.

Take, for instance, the Knapsack problem. This problem seeks to maximize the value of a knapsack (what kind of word even is knapsack?) by calculating the summation of items placed on the sack. The caveat, of course, is that there is a fixed amount of weight that the knapsack is allowed to hold. If only we lived in the Pokemon universe amiright? Anyways, give this problem to 10 different people on the street and it’s likely they would give you ten different answers, even if all the weights of the items were given to them. Give this problem to an optimization algorithm such as Genetic Algorithms or MIMIC, though, and it’ll objectively give you answers better than the average human can. This blows my mind; computation is amazing.

Even in my VIP class, I’m using machine learning to forecast the bus times in the Georgia Tech transportation system. This is actually a huge step because for the first time in nearly two semesters of being in this project, I’m doing something meaningful! But seriously, VIP is a waste of time. Anyways, small steps, but today I ran the dataset through a decision tree and it gave an astounding 76% on testing accuracy when classifying if the bus was at a stop or not. I was actually super impressed because I wasn’t sure if I completely understood how decision trees worked but after running the data, I confirmed that I must know something at least.
