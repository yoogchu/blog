makeHarvard

So, last week, 3 February 2018 - 4 February 2018, after demoing at 5?! hackathons, I won a prize at my first hackathon at MakeHarvard 2018. Ever since my first hackathon two years ago at HackFSU, whenever the demos come around, I’ve always been proud of my little creation. From a simple webapp in which I struggled iimmensely to write even 100 lines of javascript, to natural language processing, computer vision, and even hardware. Whenever demos finish I always get disappointed, you know, whenever the organizers say “those teams which have reached the final round of judging have been notified, the rest, you can pack up.” i never wanted to be ‘the rest’.

i HATED being the rest. Being the rest means that I poured my heart and soul and sacrificed sleep and a little bit of my sanity and it wasn’t good enough. No more!!


It’s actually funny; 3/4 of our team had to leave right after demos to catch a flight back to Georgia, so we never found out if we won anything or not. When we texted our last 1/4 that stayed, and he said we didn’t win anything yet, we basically gave up hope and were talking about how this would be our last hackathon, why do we do this to ourselves, etc etc etc. We went to go grab some Wendys and on the way back Joseph (our freshman ME we found at harvard, bless him) texted us that we won and it was just a whole ‘holy shit i cant believe we actually won’ moment from the moment i got the text to, ok well, i dont know about the rest of my team, but for me, until like 3 days later. it was amazing and the feeling of validation, of knowing that what you strived so hard to create wasn’t a piece of trash. it was blissful.

if theres anything i learned throughout my hackathon journey it’s that voluntary sleep means you don’t want to win enough, and if you get more than 3 hours of emergency sleep, then even your subconscious knows that you dont want to win enough.

Oh. I guess I should talk about what we actually made. It’s modular set of devices that help the visually impaired navigate a room via haptic feedback when lidar/sonar sensors sense an object in the path of the user. For demo, our prototype consisted of a headband and an ankle bracelet. We also included a raspberry pi camera that took a screenshot of the surroundings when a button is pressed and set it up to google-cloud’s vision API for image annotation. The really cool thing about it was that it could tell you general characteristics about the main stuff in the scene, for our demo purposes it spat out ‘auditorium, technology’, etc, but it could also recognize popular logos, including things like traffic signs. In our demo, we simulated walking down the street and having a stop sign present. So I walked around the auditorium aimlessly until the headband buzzed my head, letting me know that there was an obstacle in my way, such as a pole or a wall. Then I would press the button, and the image annotations could tell me things like ‘stop sign present’. Very neat, indeed.
